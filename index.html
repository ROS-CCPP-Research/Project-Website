LITERATURE SURVEY

The evolution of autonomous robotic systems, especially in the realm of Multi-Robot Systems (MRS), marks a significant stride towards achieving comprehensive space coverage, task allocation, and efficient navigation and localization. The integration of advanced path planning, map partitioning, and task distribution methodologies is imperative for enhancing the operational efficiency and adaptability of MRS in various indoor environments. Multi-robot systems have garnered considerable attention in robotics, attributed to their potential to elevate efficiency, resilience, and flexibility across diverse applications. A pivotal area of research within this domain revolves around the communication protocols and technologies that facilitate collaborative efforts among robots. An extensive examination by underscores the pivotal role of various technologies and communication paradigms in empowering multi-robot systems to tackle complex tasks effectively [1]. In the context of efficient path planning for MRS, a seminal study introduces an innovative approach for map simplification [2]. This method organizes maps into smaller, manageable segments with defined entry and exit criteria, simplifying the path planning process.

By focusing on these segments and prioritizing specific paths, the study demonstrates significant improvements in path planning efficiency, substantiated by real-world testing scenarios. A comprehensive taxonomy of task assignment strategies for MRS was outlined by Gerkey and Matari’s research, classifying them into centralized, distributed, and market-based approaches [3]. While centralized methods involve a singular entity assigning tasks, distributed approaches enable robots to collaboratively decide on task selection. Another investigation elaborates on the Consensus-Based Bundle Algorithm (CBBA), utilizing consensus algorithms for managing task allocation decisions and disseminating assigned tasks among robots [4]. This methodology achieves near-optimal task allocation with minimal communication overhead. The study of path planning techniques has resulted in important advances in the field of robot navigation, particularly in tight places. It has been demonstrated that one method, Boustrophedon cellular decomposition, efficiently reduces duplicate routes, a problem that is frequently encountered with conventional spiral traversal coverage techniques [5].

This approach is especially helpful in situations where several robots are cooperating to completely cover an area. Furthermore, studies on environmental monitoring tasks have demonstrated that the Boustrophedon approach may effectively balance productivity and coverage accuracy, especially in limited places [6]. Together, these revelations highlight the Boustrophedon approach’s supremacy in difficult conditions, representing a significant breakthrough in the field of autonomous navigation. A study proposes a cooperative navigation framework for MRS operating in linear environments, such as agricultural fields or warehouses [7]. Utilizing Directed Coverage Path Planning (DCPP), the framework aims to optimize robot exploration and task distribution, showcasing potential improvements in operational efficiency and collaboration within MRS. Additionally, Lin and Huang introduce a Collaborative Complete Coverage and Path Planning (CCPP) algorithm for both single and multi-robot systems [8]. This approach enhances coverage efficiency through a novel cost function and optimizes collaborative exploration via a goal selection function, demonstrating its effectiveness in various complex environments through simulations.

In conclusion, the burgeoning field of MRS and its applications in autonomous navigation, area coverage, and task allocation continue to evolve, driven by advancements in computational capabilities, integration of aerial and ground-based systems, and innovative approaches to resource management and energy efficiency. The collective insights from these studies lay a solid foundation for future research and development in optimizing the deployment and coordination of MRS in complex, dynamic environments. Together, these studies provide insightful information about the advancements and difficulties encountered in the field of multi-robot systems, emphasizing the need for creative solutions that improve communications and cooperation between robots and people as well as between robots.

RESEARCH GAP

Despite significant advancements in multi-robot systems, several research gaps remain. Current algorithms for map partitioning often fail to efficiently segment complex environments, leading to suboptimal coverage. Path planning methods such as Spiral Traversal Coverage (STC) frequently encounter issues with path redundancy, particularly in narrow areas, necessitating hybrid approaches that are not yet fully developed. Additionally, task allocation mechanisms lack the ability to dynamically adjust to changing numbers of robots and varying task complexities, resulting in inefficient workload distribution. Moreover, existing systems struggle with autonomous navigation and accurate localization within the given area, which hinders the robots' ability to navigate and cover the environment effectively. Addressing these gaps is crucial for enhancing the effectiveness and reliability of multi-robot systems in complex indoor environments.

RESEARCH PROBLEM & SOLUTION

How can we create an efficient multi-robot path coverage system that divides a map into a matrix, fairly distributes tasks among robots, reduces redundant paths in narrow areas, reliably allocates tasks, navigation methods and adapts to relevant given environment?

Designing an efficient multi-robot path coverage system involves several key challenges. First, we need to create an algorithm that divides the overall map into a matrix representation for effective path planning. This algorithm should segment the environment into manageable cells, each correctly sized and positioned to optimize coverage while accounting for obstacles and free spaces. This precise mapping allows robots to navigate and cover the area effectively. Additionally, developing a path coverage algorithm that ensures fair task distribution among a changing number of robots is essential. By combining different techniques like Spiral Traversal Coverage (STC) and Boustrophedon Traversal Coverage (BTC), we can minimize redundant paths in narrow areas and balance the coverage workload among all robots.

This critical aspect is establishing a reliable and efficient task allocation mechanism between the system and the robots, localization and navigate through implemented given paths to cover the assign area. This requires a market-based or auction-based system where tasks are assigned based on each robot's capabilities, task complexity, and proximity. Such a mechanism ensures that tasks are distributed fairly and efficiently, considering the dynamic nature of the robot fleet and varying task difficulties. Finally, the system must adapt to real-time changes in the environment, allowing robots to update their strategies and maintain effective coverage continuously. And then we need to ensure robots can accurately determine their position and navigate within the environment. Using tools like the robot localization package and precise path tracking methods, robots can autonomously cover designated areas effectively.

Main Objective

Optimize area coverage techniques for comprehensive and adaptable path coverage in virtual environments through efficient map partitioning, path planning, task allocation and autonomous navigation in multi-robot systems.

METHODOLOGY

Our methodology focuses on developing and implementing an integrated system to improve the functionality and performance of multi-robot systems in indoor environments. This involves creating algorithms and techniques for navigation, localization, task allocation, map segmentation, and path planning.

Map Partitioning: We begin by acquiring an occupancy grid map, which represents the environment with free and occupied spaces. The map is then partitioned using a cell-decomposition approach to ensure each robot covers different parts efficiently, minimizing overlap. The environment is divided into smaller, manageable polygons or cells. These cells are assigned to robots based on the number of robots and their coverage tools. The map is trimmed to focus on essential areas, and a threshold is set to determine whether a cell is occupied or free.

Path Planning: Path planning is crucial for multi-robot systems to achieve complete coverage. We employ grid-based and graph-based methods to generate effective coverage plans. Our approach combines Spiral Traversal Coverage (STC) and Boustrophedon Traversal Coverage (BTC) to address path redundancy issues. We use BFS algorithms to identify narrow areas in the partitions and apply hybrid path planning, switching between STC and BTC as needed.

Task Allocation: Task allocation involves assigning tasks to robots based on their capabilities and the complexity of the tasks. Using a market-based algorithm, tasks are auctioned to robots, and bids are evaluated based on task priority, complexity, and distance. A central entity, Queen Bot, oversees this process, ensuring tasks are assigned to the most suitable robots. This method ensures efficient and effective task distribution.

Localization and Navigation: Localization and navigation are essential for optimizing area coverage. We use the TurtleBot3 robot models and the robot_localization package, which employs the Unscented Kalman Filter (UKF) or Extended Kalman Filter (EKF) to fuse data from multiple sensors. This setup enables robots to localize accurately and navigate their environment autonomously. Robots are initialized in a simulated environment with unique IDs. The system is tested with a single robot before scaling to a multi-robot setup. Each robot follows the path plan generated during the task allocation process, subscribing to the /path topic for complete area coverage. We use the "tracking_pid" package for precise trajectory tracking, ensuring robots accurately follow their assigned paths.

Our hybrid strategy combining STC and BTC reduces path redundancy and ensures efficient coverage, especially in narrow areas. After path planning, all paths are merged and published as a single plan. This comprehensive approach leverages multiple robots to achieve thorough and efficient area coverage, demonstrating the effectiveness of our integrated system.
